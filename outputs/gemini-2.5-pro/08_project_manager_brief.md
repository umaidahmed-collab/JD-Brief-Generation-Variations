### **Part 2: The Ideal Candidate – What to Look For**

This is your cheat sheet for identifying A-level talent.

#### **The Must-Haves (Your Screening Checklist):**

*   **Large-Scale Project Experience:** The candidate **must** have been the lead Project Manager on commercial construction projects valued at **over $20 million**. This is non-negotiable. Managing a $5M project is not the same; the financial risk, team size, and complexity are worlds apart.
    *   **Screening Question:** "Tell me about the largest, most complex project you have managed from start to finish. What was the dollar value, and what was your exact role?"
*   **Full Lifecycle Experience:** They need to have seen projects through from the very beginning (pre-construction) to the very end (closeout). They can't just be a "construction phase" manager.
    *   **Screening Question:** "Walk me through your involvement in the pre-construction process. Have you been involved in bidding, buying out subcontractors, and contract negotiation?"
*   **Commercial Construction Background:** Their experience must be in **commercial** construction. This means buildings like offices, hospitals, schools, or labs. Experience in residential (houses, apartments) or heavy civil (roads, bridges) is **not** a fit.
*   **Leadership, Not Just Management:** They must have experience directly managing a team (e.g., Assistant PMs, Project Engineers).
    *   **Screening Question:** "Describe the team you managed on your last major project. Who reported to you?"
*   **Based in or Willing to Relocate to Phoenix:** This is an in-person role with significant site travel.

#### **Explaining the Jargon (Key Terms in Plain English):**

*   **Primavera P6 / Microsoft Project:** These are sophisticated scheduling software. Think of them as the master blueprint for the project's *timeline*. It details every single task, who does it, and when. Proficiency here is key.
*   **Procore / Bluebeam:** Procore is a project management platform—the central hub for all project communication, documents, and financials. Bluebeam is a tool used for viewing and marking up digital blueprints (PDFs). They are the digital toolbox for a modern PM.
*   **AIA / ConsensusDocs:** These are the industry-standard templates for contracts between the owner, the architect, and the contractor (us). Knowing them is like a lawyer knowing how to read a legal document. It's fundamental.
*   **OAC Meetings (Owner, Architect, Contractor):** This is the weekly leadership meeting for the project. The PM leads this meeting, so they need to be a confident and clear communicator.
*   **Change Orders, RFIs, Submittals:**
    *   **Change Order:** When the client wants to change something after the plan is set, it creates a "change order" which adjusts the price and schedule. The PM manages this financially and logistically.
    *   **RFI (Request for Information):** If the construction team finds a problem or ambiguity in the blueprints, they submit an RFI to the architect for clarification. The PM manages this flow of questions and answers.
    *   **Submittal:** The process of getting architect approval for the exact materials being used (e.g., "Here is the specific type of window we plan to install, please approve it.").
*   **Design-Build / CM-at-Risk:** These are types of contracts. Instead of the traditional model, these involve the construction company getting involved much earlier in the design phase. Experience here is a huge plus as it shows they can collaborate with architects and manage risk from day one.

#### **The "Perfect" Candidate Profile (The Intangibles):**

You're not just looking for a technical expert. You're looking for a specific type of person. The ideal candidate:
*   Is a **poised and polished communicator**. They can confidently speak with a demanding hospital CEO one minute and a subcontractor on the job site the next. They don't get flustered.
*   Is **financially savvy**. They treat the project budget like it's their own money. They can spot financial risks from a mile away and aren't afraid to have tough conversations about money.
*   Is a **problem-solver, not a problem-reporter**. When challenges arise, their first instinct is to find a solution, not to make excuses.
*   Has **"calm authority."** They lead with respect and expertise, not by yelling. People trust them and want to work for them.

#### **Red Flags (What to Avoid):**

*   **Job Hoppers:** Someone who has had 4 jobs in 6 years is a major risk. We need stability.
*   **"Project Engineers" or "Assistant PMs" only:** If they haven't held the title of **Project Manager** with primary responsibility for a large project, they are not ready for this senior role.
*   **Wrong Industry:** Candidates from residential, industrial manufacturing, or IT project management are not a fit, no matter how senior they are. The context is completely different.
*   **Blames Others:** When you ask about a project that went wrong, do they take ownership or do they blame the client, the architect, or their team? A-players take responsibility.
